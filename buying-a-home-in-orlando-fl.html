Where the Orlando Market Stands Right Now

Per the Orlando Regional Realtor Association, the metro median sale price has been running roughly $370,000 to $400,000 through 2026, up modestly year over year after a run of rapid appreciation from 2021 to 2023. The market has shifted toward more balanced conditions, meaning more inventory and less of the multiple-offer chaos that defined the pandemic-era market.

Orlando is not one price point. It is a wide metro spanning Orange, Osceola, Seminole, and Lake counties, and the range between submarkets is significant, from mid-$300,000s in parts of Kissimmee to $600,000 and above in Dr. Phillips and Windermere.

For buyers, that spread is an opportunity. Knowing which submarket fits your budget and commute matters more here than in a smaller, more uniform market.

Orlando Neighborhoods Worth Knowing Before You Buy

The Orlando metro is not one market. It is several, each with a different price point, commute profile, and buyer demographic.

Lake Nona in southeast Orlando has a median price around $480,000, driven by the Medical City healthcare corridor and steady employer growth. Strong pick for healthcare workers and buyers who want new construction in a master-planned community.

Kissimmee and Celebration to the south run more affordable, roughly $335,000 to $360,000 for comparable homes. Close to the attractions corridor and popular with hospitality workers and families who want more home for the money.

Winter Garden, Horizon West, and Oviedo sit in the $400,000 to $600,000 range for newer 4-bedroom, 2.5-bath homes with strong school zoning. These are the areas I point families toward first when schools are the top priority.

Winter Park runs higher, with a median around $540,000. Established, walkable, close to downtown, and popular with buyers who want character over new construction.

Dr. Phillips and Windermere are the upper tier of the metro, with homes commonly starting at $600,000 and climbing well past that near the water or in gated communities.

I can help you figure out which submarket makes sense for your commute, budget, and what you are actually looking for in a home.

What the Homebuying Process Looks Like in Florida

Buying a home in Florida follows the same general sequence as most states, but there are a few Florida-specific things worth knowing before you start.

First, Florida has a homestead exemption that takes $50,000 off your home's assessed value for property taxes, as long as it is your primary residence. That typically saves $500 to $1,000 per year on your tax bill.

Florida also has Save Our Homes, which caps annual property tax increases at 3% or the consumer price index once you are homesteaded. This is meaningful if you plan to stay long-term in a metro where values keep rising.

Florida homeowners insurance is more expensive than most states. This has gotten worse since 2022 due to hurricane risk and insurer exits from the market. Budget for this separately and factor it into your total monthly payment, especially in Orlando's newer, larger homes.

HOA fees are common in Central Florida developments, especially newer communities in Lake Nona, Horizon West, and Winter Garden. Always ask upfront what the HOA covers and review the budget before making an offer.

The purchase timeline from accepted offer to closing typically runs 30 to 45 days. Cash purchases can close faster. I walk you through each step so there are no surprises.

Who Buys Homes in Orlando (and Why)

The Orlando buyer market is broad, but a few segments show up most often.

Out-of-state relocators from the Northeast, Midwest, and South Florida make up a large share. No state income tax and a lower cost of living than the coasts are the consistent draw, even with Orlando's own prices climbing in recent years.

Healthcare workers and researchers gravitate toward Lake Nona and the Medical City corridor, where employer proximity outweighs a higher price point.

Families prioritize school zoning above almost everything else, which pulls them toward Winter Garden, Horizon West, and Oviedo even when those areas cost more than closer-in alternatives.

Hospitality and attractions-industry workers tend to look at Kissimmee, Celebration, and Davenport, where prices stay more accessible and the commute to the theme park corridor is short.
