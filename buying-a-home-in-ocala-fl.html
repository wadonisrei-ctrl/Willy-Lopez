Why Ocala Is One of the Best Places to Buy Right Now

Ocala is one of the more affordable homebuying markets left in Florida. Recent market reports put the Marion County median in the $270,000 to $305,000 range, well below comparable homes in Orlando or Tampa.

That gap has kept demand steady as more people discover Ocala. The World Equestrian Center opened in 2021 and put Ocala on a national map it had never been on before. The Ocala metro has also been named the fastest-growing metro in the country in recent U.S. Census Bureau population estimates.

For first-time buyers, remote workers, retirees, and people moving from high-cost markets in the Northeast or South Florida, Ocala offers something rare: a real path to homeownership at a price that makes financial sense.

Ocala Neighborhoods Worth Knowing Before You Buy

Ocala is not one market. It is several, each with a different price point, commute profile, and buyer demographic.

Silver Springs Shores is the largest affordable neighborhood in Ocala. Entry-level single-family homes run $200,000 to $270,000. High demand, high turnover, strong rental yield if investment is part of the goal.

Marion Oaks in SW Ocala is another entry-level area with similar price ranges. Popular with first-time buyers and investors.

SE Ocala along the 17th Street corridor has newer construction and sits close to both major hospitals. Prices range from $280,000 to $380,000. This is where healthcare workers and younger professionals tend to buy.

Paddock Park and NW Ocala are the mid-to-upper tier. Larger lots, more established neighborhoods, prices from $350,000 to $500,000 and above.

Downtown Ocala has condos and townhomes in the $180,000 to $320,000 range. Walkable, actively revitalized, and attracts a younger professional buyer.

I can help you figure out which neighborhood makes sense for your commute, budget, and what you are actually looking for in a home.

What the Homebuying Process Looks Like in Florida

Buying a home in Florida follows the same general sequence as most states, but there are a few Florida-specific things worth knowing before you start.

First, Florida has a homestead exemption that takes $50,000 off your home's assessed value for property taxes, as long as it is your primary residence. That typically saves $500 to $1,000 per year on your tax bill.

Florida also has Save Our Homes, which caps annual property tax increases at 3% or the consumer price index once you are homesteaded. This is meaningful if you plan to stay long-term and values keep rising.

Florida homeowners insurance is more expensive than most states. This has gotten worse since 2022 due to hurricane risk and insurer exits from the market. Budget for this separately and factor it into your total monthly payment.

HOA fees are common in Central Florida developments, especially newer communities. Always ask upfront what the HOA covers and review the budget before making an offer.

The purchase timeline from accepted offer to closing typically runs 30 to 45 days. Cash purchases can close faster. I walk you through each step so there are no surprises.

Who Buys Homes in Ocala (and Why)

The Ocala buyer market is more diverse than people expect.

Retirees and pre-retirees from the Northeast and Midwest make up the largest segment. They sell a $600,000 home in New Jersey and buy a $280,000 home in Ocala debt-free. The math is hard to argue with.

Families priced out of Orlando and Tampa have been moving to Ocala in large numbers since 2020. The commute to Orlando is about an hour. For remote workers, that commute does not matter at all.

Healthcare workers at Advent Health Ocala and HCA Florida Ocala buy here because it is where they work. Both hospitals are on SW 1st Avenue, which makes the commute from most Ocala neighborhoods manageable.

First-time buyers who could not afford Central Florida metro prices find Ocala offers genuine entry-level inventory under $280,000 that still exists in a state where that has become rare.
