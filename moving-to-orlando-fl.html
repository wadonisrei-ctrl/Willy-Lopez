Why People Move to Orlando

Most people moving to Orlando are coming from one of three places: the Northeast, South Florida, or the Midwest. The reasons are usually the same. Florida has no state income tax, which by itself makes a real difference for middle-income earners. Orlando rents run well below Miami, Fort Lauderdale, and Boca Raton. And the weather in Central Florida is more forgiving than coastal South Florida during hurricane season.

The other big driver is remote work. Since 2020, a lot of people who can work from anywhere chose Orlando because it has the infrastructure of a real city, reasonable cost of living, and easy access to both coasts via I-4 and two major airports. You can fly direct to almost anywhere from Orlando International.

If you are coming from New York or New Jersey specifically, the price shock goes the other direction in the best way. A two-bedroom in Orlando rents for what you might pay for a one-bedroom in Hoboken. That difference adds up fast.

How Orlando Compares to Other Florida Cities

Orlando sits in the middle of Florida, which is both a strength and a limitation. You are about 85 miles from Tampa, 55 miles from Daytona Beach, and 235 miles from Miami. If a beach day requires a drive, that is the tradeoff for living somewhere with lower housing costs and less traffic than South Florida.

Compared to Tampa, Orlando has a larger rental inventory and rents in a similar range. Compared to Jacksonville, Orlando has more employment anchors in healthcare, tourism, and tech. Compared to Ocala, Orlando is more urban with more options but noticeably higher prices.

Most people who contact me and say they are moving to Orlando are actually moving to the greater metro area. That includes communities in Orange, Seminole, Osceola, and Lake counties. Lake Nona, Kissimmee, Davenport, Oviedo, Winter Garden, and Dr. Phillips are all technically outside the city limits but fully part of the Orlando market.

Orlando Neighborhoods Worth Knowing Before You Search

The Orlando rental market is spread across a wide footprint. Most apartment communities are not downtown. Understanding the submarkets before you search saves a lot of confusion. The ranges below are what I am seeing in the market right now, not a formal study, so treat them as a starting point and ask me for current availability when you are ready to look.

Lake Nona is in southeast Orlando near the airport. It is a master-planned community built around Medical City, a cluster of hospitals and research institutions. If you are a healthcare worker, this is the first place to look. One-bedrooms typically run $1,400 to $1,800 per month.

Kissimmee is south of Orlando along US-192 and is close to Disney World and the attractions corridor. Rents are lower than central Orlando and the commute to downtown is manageable. A lot of Disney cast members and hospitality workers live here. One-bedrooms range from $1,300 to $1,700.

Davenport is southwest of Kissimmee and offers some of the best pricing in the metro for newer construction. It is less urban than Kissimmee but less than a 30-minute drive from the major employers near Disney. One-bedrooms run $1,300 to $1,500.

Millenia and Dr. Phillips are near the Mall at Millenia on the west side of Orlando. Good restaurant access, close to Universal Studios, and a major hospital cluster along Sand Lake Road. One-bedrooms run $1,500 to $1,800 here.

Downtown Orlando is for people who want walkability and urban energy. It is a small downtown by major city standards but has grown significantly in the last decade with new high-rises. Expect $1,600 to $2,200 for a one-bedroom in newer construction.

Oviedo is a suburb northeast of Orlando near UCF. Quieter, family-oriented, and about 30 minutes from downtown. One-bedrooms run $1,500 to $1,800 in newer communities.

Winter Garden is 20 minutes west of downtown and has a historic downtown district that is walkable and popular with young families. Good schools and an active real estate market. Apartments run $1,400 to $1,700 for a one-bedroom.

Your Florida Move-In Checklist

Once you have a move date, a few Florida-specific tasks need to happen that people moving within-state do not think about. Florida requires new residents to get a Florida driver's license and register any vehicles, generally within 30 days of establishing residency. The FLHSMV office nearest your new address handles both, and a signed lease is normally accepted as proof of residency.

Utilities depend on exactly where you land in the metro. Inside Orlando city limits, both electric and water run through OUC, the Orlando Utilities Commission, a city-owned utility. In Kissimmee, it is Kissimmee Utility Authority. Elsewhere in the metro, electric is typically Duke Energy with city or county water. Your leasing office can confirm the exact provider for a specific address.

Update your address with USPS mail forwarding, your bank, and any subscriptions before the move, not after. If you want to register to vote in Florida, your county's Supervisor of Elections office, Orange, Seminole, Osceola, or Lake depending on where you land, handles that once you have a Florida address.

None of this needs to happen before you arrive. It needs to happen in the first 30 days after you do. The one thing worth handling before you arrive is the apartment itself, so you are not doing all of this from a hotel room.

Your Moving Timeline: What to Handle and When

6 to 8 weeks out: narrow down a submarket and budget. This is when I usually hear from people first. Tell me your move date, budget, and bedroom count, and I will match you against the communities I work with across the metro.

4 weeks out: get your apartment locked in. Once you pick a community, you can usually complete a video walkthrough, apply, and get approved remotely. Most approvals come back within 24 to 48 hours. Book your moving company or truck rental now if you have not already, since good movers book up fast in summer.

2 weeks out: start the address change list. USPS mail forwarding, banks, subscriptions, and any medical providers who need records transferred.

Moving week: if you can control the date, avoid arriving on a Friday afternoon or the same weekend as a major theme park event. I-4 through Orlando is genuinely one of the more congested stretches of highway in the state, and it gets worse around holidays. Keep your lease, ID, and proof of income documents with you, not packed in the moving truck.

First 30 days after arrival: Florida driver's license, vehicle registration, and voter registration if you want it done. See the checklist above for where to go.

I only handle one piece of this personally, the apartment. But getting that piece locked in early takes the biggest source of moving stress off the table before you deal with everything else.

Cost of Living: Orlando vs Where You're Coming From

Rent is the number most people fixate on, but it is not the only cost that shifts when you move to Central Florida. No state income tax is the headline difference, and it is a real, immediate raise compared to states like New York, New Jersey, or Illinois.

Home and auto insurance in Florida run higher than almost anywhere else in the country, largely because of statewide hurricane and flood risk, and Orlando is no exception even sitting inland. Budget for this before you sign a lease, not after your first renewal notice. Renters insurance itself is usually inexpensive, often under $20 a month, and many of the communities I work with require it.

Orlando's job market leans heavily on tourism and hospitality, which means a wide range of wage levels in one metro, from theme park and hotel jobs to the healthcare, aerospace, and tech employers clustered around Lake Nona and the research corridor. Groceries and everyday goods track close to the national average. Gas prices follow the statewide average, though expect more time in the car than a smaller city, since the metro is spread out.
