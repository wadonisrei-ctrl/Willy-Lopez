Why People Move to Orlando

Most people moving to Orlando are coming from one of three places: the Northeast, South Florida, or the Midwest. The reasons are usually the same. Florida has no state income tax, which alone saves thousands per year for middle-income earners. Orlando rents run 30 to 50 percent below Miami, Fort Lauderdale, and Boca Raton. And the weather in Central Florida is more forgiving than coastal South Florida during hurricane season.

The other big driver is remote work. Since 2020, a lot of people who can work from anywhere chose Orlando because it has the infrastructure of a real city, reasonable cost of living, and easy access to both coasts via I-4 and two major airports. You can fly direct to almost anywhere from Orlando International.

If you are coming from New York or New Jersey specifically, the price shock goes the other direction in the best way. A two-bedroom in Orlando rents for what you might pay for a one-bedroom in Hoboken. That difference adds up fast.

How Orlando Compares to Other Florida Cities

Orlando sits in the middle of Florida, which is both a strength and a limitation. You are about 75 miles from Tampa, 60 miles from Daytona Beach, and 225 miles from Miami. If a beach day requires a drive, that is the tradeoff for living somewhere with significantly lower housing costs and no traffic as bad as South Florida.

Compared to Tampa, Orlando has a larger rental inventory and slightly lower average rents in most neighborhoods. Compared to Jacksonville, Orlando has more employment anchors in healthcare, tourism, and tech. Compared to Ocala, Orlando is more urban with more options but noticeably higher prices.

Most people who contact me and say they are moving to Orlando are actually moving to the greater metro area. That includes communities in Orange, Seminole, Osceola, and Lake counties. Lake Nona, Kissimmee, Davenport, Oviedo, Winter Garden, and Dr. Phillips are all technically outside the city limits but fully part of the Orlando market.

Orlando Neighborhoods Worth Knowing Before You Search

The Orlando rental market is spread across a wide footprint. Most apartment communities are not downtown. Understanding the submarkets before you search saves a lot of confusion.

Lake Nona is in southeast Orlando near the airport. It is a master-planned community built around Medical City, a cluster of hospitals and research institutions. If you are a healthcare worker, this is the first place to look. One-bedrooms typically run $1,400 to $1,800 per month.

Kissimmee is south of Orlando along US-192 and is close to Disney World and the attractions corridor. Rents are lower than central Orlando and the commute to downtown is manageable. A lot of Disney cast members and hospitality workers live here. One-bedrooms range from $1,300 to $1,700.

Davenport is southwest of Kissimmee and offers some of the best pricing in the metro for newer construction. It is less urban than Kissimmee but less than a 30-minute drive from the major employers near Disney. One-bedrooms run $1,300 to $1,500.

Millenia and Dr. Phillips are near the Mall at Millenia on the west side of Orlando. Good restaurant access, close to Universal Studios, and a major hospital cluster along Sand Lake Road. One-bedrooms run $1,500 to $1,800 here.

Downtown Orlando is for people who want walkability and urban energy. It is a small downtown by major city standards but has grown significantly in the last decade with new high-rises. Expect $1,600 to $2,200 for a one-bedroom in newer construction.

Oviedo is a suburb northeast of Orlando near UCF. Quieter, family-oriented, and about 30 minutes from downtown. One-bedrooms run $1,500 to $1,800 in newer communities.

Winter Garden is 20 minutes west of downtown and has a historic downtown district that is walkable and popular with young families. Good schools and an active real estate market. Apartments run $1,400 to $1,700 for a one-bedroom.

What to Know About the Orlando Rental Market Before You Move

Orlando is not a cheap city by Florida standards. It used to be, but significant in-migration since 2020 pushed rents up across the metro. The market has stabilized since its 2022 to 2023 peak but has not returned to pre-pandemic levels.

A realistic budget for a one-bedroom in a newer construction community anywhere in the metro is $1,400 to $1,900 per month. Two-bedrooms run $1,700 to $2,400 depending on location and amenities. Older communities are cheaper but tend to have higher maintenance frustrations.

Move-in costs are usually one to two months of rent upfront. That typically includes a deposit and first month. Some communities offer reduced deposits for strong applicants. A few waive the deposit entirely during lease-up specials on new construction.

For income requirements, most communities require gross monthly income of 2.5 to 3 times the monthly rent. If you are a remote worker, freelancer, or otherwise self-employed, bring 2 to 3 months of bank statements. Communities accept those in place of traditional pay stubs.

If you have a pet, expect a non-refundable pet fee of $200 to $500 and a monthly pet rent of $25 to $75 per pet. Most communities have weight limits, usually 50 to 75 pounds, and restrict certain breeds. I filter for pet-friendly communities based on your specific pet.
