FSBO vs Real Estate Agent in Florida: An Honest Comparison

Most FSBO sellers save less than they expect. Here is why, and when it might still make sense.

Why People Try FSBO in Florida

The appeal of selling your home without an agent is straightforward: you avoid paying listing commission. On a $300,000 home with a 3% listing commission, that is $9,000 you keep.

That is real money. The question is what you give up to keep it, and whether the tradeoff works out in your favor.

FSBO is most common when sellers know their buyer (a family member, neighbor, or existing tenant buying the property), when the market is so hot that anything sells regardless of marketing, or when the seller has significant real estate experience themselves.

For everyone else, the math usually comes out closer than sellers expect.

What FSBO Actually Costs in Florida

Selling without an agent does not mean selling without costs. Here is what FSBO sellers in Florida still pay.

Florida disclosure requirements are mandatory regardless of whether you have an agent. You are legally required to disclose known material defects. If you miss something and the buyer finds out after closing, you are exposed to legal action. Many FSBO sellers hire a real estate attorney to review their disclosures, which runs $500 to $1,500.

Marketing costs are real. Without MLS access, FSBO homes get significantly less exposure. You can pay $300 to $500 for a flat-fee MLS listing service, plus photography ($150 to $400), a yard sign, and your own time for showings and follow-up.

Buyer's agent commission is still expected in most Florida transactions. Even post-NAR settlement, most buyers working with an agent expect their agent to be compensated. If you refuse to offer buyer's agent compensation, you shrink your buyer pool significantly, which reduces competition and typically your final sale price.

Florida requires a closing agent (title company or attorney). This cost is the same whether you have an agent or not.

Total FSBO overhead: $1,500 to $4,000 before accounting for time, pricing errors, and lost sale price.

What You Actually Give Up Without an Agent

The commission savings only make sense if you sell for the same price you would have gotten with an agent. According to NAR's annual Profile of Home Buyers and Sellers, FSBO homes consistently sell for less than agent-listed homes, with the gap typically running 5 to 15% depending on the year and the market.

On a $300,000 home, a 5% gap is $15,000. That exceeds what you would have paid in listing commission.

FSBO sellers face several real disadvantages beyond marketing reach.

Pricing is hard without access to the right tools. Automated estimates like Zillow's Zestimate are notoriously inaccurate. Many FSBO sellers overprice initially (because they are emotionally attached to the home) and then chase the market down with price reductions. Homes with price reductions attract lower offers and longer days on market.

Negotiation is harder without a professional buffer. Buyers and their agents know how to negotiate. Most sellers are doing it once. Having an experienced agent in the middle changes the dynamic in your favor.

Inspection negotiations catch many FSBO sellers off guard. When the inspector finds issues (and they always find something), buyers submit repair requests. Knowing what to accept, what to counter, and what to push back on takes experience.

Transaction management has a lot of moving parts. Deadlines, contingencies, title issues, HOA approvals, and last-minute lender requests all need coordination. Missing a deadline can kill a deal.

When FSBO Actually Makes Sense

There are situations where FSBO is reasonable.

You already know your buyer. If a family member, neighbor, friend, or tenant wants to buy your property, FSBO makes sense. You still need a title company and a real estate attorney to review the contract and disclosures, but the marketing problem is already solved.

The market is extremely hot and anything sells. In a true seller's market with multiple offers on every home, the agent's marketing advantage matters less. This was more true in 2021 and 2022 than it is now.

You have real estate experience. If you work in real estate, have sold homes before, or understand contracts and negotiations, you can manage the process yourself. Most people do not have this background.

For most sellers in Ocala and Orlando right now, the math favors an agent, particularly when you factor in proper pricing, MLS exposure, and negotiation support.

The NAR Settlement Changed Some of the Equation

In August 2024, the National Association of Realtors settled a lawsuit that changed how buyer agent compensation works. Under the old model, sellers typically paid a total commission that covered both the listing agent and the buyer's agent. Under the new rules, buyer agent compensation is negotiated separately.

This does not make FSBO meaningfully easier or cheaper. Buyers working with agents still expect their agent to be paid, and the negotiation around that still happens. FSBO sellers still face this conversation.

What the settlement did change is that commission structures are now more explicitly negotiable for all sellers, whether they use an agent or not. This is worth a conversation before you list.
