The Order of Operations

Most first-time buyers in Florida make the same mistake: they start looking at homes before they know what they can afford or what their loan options are. That leads to disappointment, wasted time, and sometimes falling in love with a house that is $40,000 above what the bank will actually lend.

Here is the right order:

1. Check your credit score. You want at least 620 for most conventional loans. FHA loans accept 580 with a 3.5% down payment. Get your credit report at annualcreditreport.com and look for any errors worth disputing before you apply.

2. Get pre-approved. Not pre-qualified, which is a rough estimate. Pre-approved means a lender has verified your income, assets, and credit. Sellers in Orlando and Ocala expect a pre-approval letter with any offer.

3. Know your full payment. Your mortgage is not your only monthly cost. Add property taxes, homeowners insurance, and HOA fees (if the community has one). In Central Florida, all three together can add $600 to $1,200 per month on top of your principal and interest.

4. Find the right home in the right area. Now you can shop with confidence because you know exactly what you can afford and what the true monthly cost looks like.

Florida First-Time Buyer Programs Worth Knowing

Florida has several programs specifically designed to help first-time buyers with down payment and closing costs.

Florida Housing Finance Corporation (Florida Housing) offers down payment assistance programs available through participating lenders. These programs give qualified buyers $10,000 or more toward their down payment, with low or no-interest terms. Income limits apply and vary by county.

Orange County and Marion County both have their own local assistance programs in some years, depending on federal grant funding. These are worth asking your lender about specifically.

FHA loans are the most commonly used loan type for first-time buyers in Florida. They require 3.5% down with a 580 credit score. Mortgage insurance is required until you reach 20% equity, but the lower barrier to entry makes them popular.

VA loans are available to veterans and active military with no down payment required. If you have served, this is usually the best option available.

USDA loans work for homes in certain rural areas, including parts of Marion County around Ocala. No down payment required, income limits apply.

Florida-Specific Costs First-Time Buyers Often Miss

Florida has a few costs that catch out-of-state buyers off guard.

Homeowners insurance in Florida is expensive, and it has gotten more expensive since 2022 as national insurers have reduced their Florida exposure. For a $280,000 home in Ocala, budget $2,000 to $3,500 per year for insurance. Coastal and flood-zone properties cost significantly more. Always get an insurance quote before making an offer.

HOA fees are common in Central Florida. Many newer developments require them. Some are $100 to $200 per month. Others run $400 to $600. Always find out what the HOA fee is and what it covers before you fall in love with a house.

Documentary stamp tax on the deed is paid by the seller in Florida, but documentary stamps on the mortgage note are paid by the buyer. On a $300,000 loan, that is about $1,050. This shows up in your closing costs.

The homestead exemption can save you real money every year. Apply for it through your county property appraiser after closing. It removes $50,000 from your home's assessed value for tax purposes.

Why Ocala Is Worth Considering for First-Time Buyers

The biggest challenge for first-time buyers in Florida right now is price. Orlando and Tampa are competitive and expensive. For many first-time buyers, the inventory that fits their budget is in Ocala.

Ocala offers real entry-level inventory. Homes in Silver Springs Shores and Marion Oaks start around $200,000 to $270,000. That is real homeownership at a price that works for a lot of buyers who would be priced out in Orange County.

The tradeoff is location. Ocala is about 90 minutes from downtown Orlando. For remote workers, that does not matter. For people who commute to Orlando, it may. But for buyers who work locally in Ocala, healthcare, retail, or logistics, it is a very strong market to enter.
