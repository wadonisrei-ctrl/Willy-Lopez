Best Time to Sell a Home in Florida

Spring gets the headlines nationally. Florida's own calendar, snowbird season and hurricane season, changes the picture in ways worth knowing before you list.

What the National Data Actually Shows

ATTOM Data Solutions analyzed more than 52 million single-family home and condo sales from 2015 through 2025 and found that seller premiums (the percentage a home sells above its estimated market value) peak in spring. March led at a 10.7% premium, followed by April and May at 10.2% each. Premiums decline steadily through the second half of the year, bottoming out in the fall, with October the weakest month at a 7.9% premium.

That data is national, not Florida-specific. There is no widely published state-by-state breakdown from ATTOM or similar sources, so treat the spring pattern as a real, broad trend rather than a Florida guarantee. What it does tell you is that buyer competition and urgency both tend to build earlier in the year and ease off in the back half, which lines up with what I see in Central Florida too.

Why Florida's Calendar Is Different

Florida has two seasonal forces that do not show up in national data: snowbird season and hurricane season.

Snowbird season runs roughly January through April, when seasonal residents and retirees from the Northeast and Midwest are physically in Florida and shopping for homes. In Central Florida, I see buyer traffic pick up noticeably during this window, especially for retiree-friendly communities and 55+ properties. This overlaps with the front half of the national spring premium window, which is part of why spring works particularly well here.

Hurricane season runs June 1 through November 30. It does not stop the market, but it changes buyer psychology. Buyers get more cautious about flood zones, roof age, and insurance costs during active storm months, and showings can get disrupted by an actual storm threat. Listings that go up in late summer or early fall sometimes sit longer for reasons that have nothing to do with pricing. This lines up with the national data showing September and October as historically weaker months, but in Florida the hurricane factor adds a second, more direct reason on top of the general seasonal slowdown.

What This Means If You Are Deciding When to List

If your timeline is flexible, listing between February and May gives you the best combination of national seasonal tailwind and Florida's snowbird buyer traffic. This is when I see the most serious, motivated buyers move through the market.

If you need to sell in the second half of the year, it does not mean your home will not sell. It means pricing accuracy matters even more, since there is less foot traffic to absorb a pricing mistake. I also make sure your listing addresses the questions storm-season buyers are asking upfront: roof age and material, flood zone designation, and current insurance costs. Answering those before a buyer has to ask keeps showings from stalling.

If you do not have flexibility on timing, the honest answer is that a well-priced, well-presented home sells in any month in Central Florida. Seasonality shifts the odds, it does not decide the outcome.
