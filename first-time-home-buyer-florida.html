Florida First-Time Buyer Programs Worth Knowing

Florida Housing Finance Corporation (Florida Housing) runs the main statewide programs for first-time buyers.

The HFA Preferred and HFA Advantage programs offer 30-year fixed-rate mortgages at below-market interest rates for qualified buyers. You do not have to be a first-time buyer in the strictest sense. Florida defines a first-time buyer as someone who has not owned a primary residence in the past 3 years.

Down payment assistance is available through two programs layered on top of the mortgage:

Florida Assist provides up to $10,000 in down payment and closing cost help as a 0% interest, no monthly payment second mortgage. It is repaid when you sell, refinance, or pay off the first mortgage.

Florida HLP provides down payment assistance at 3% interest with a monthly payment of roughly $50 to $55. This one fully amortizes over 15 years. The exact loan cap has moved around in recent years, so I confirm the current figure with your lender before you apply.

Separately, Florida's Hometown Heroes program offers larger assistance, up to $35,000, for full-time workers in eligible professions such as healthcare, education, law enforcement, and the military, structured as a 0% interest deferred second mortgage. It cannot be combined with Florida Assist, so your lender will help you pick whichever program fits your situation better.

Income and purchase price limits apply and vary by county. In Orange County (Orlando), the limit is higher than in Marion County (Ocala) because incomes are higher. Your lender applies for these programs when you apply for your mortgage.

For service members and veterans, the Florida First Military program offers a lower interest rate as an additional benefit.

The Florida Homestead Exemption: Your First Big Tax Break

If you buy a home in Florida and use it as your primary residence, you qualify for the homestead exemption. File by March 1st of the year following your purchase.

Here is what it does. First, it reduces your home's assessed value by $25,000 for all taxing authorities, and another $25,000 for all non-school taxing authorities. Combined, that is up to $50,000 off your assessed value.

On a $270,000 home in Ocala, you might be taxed on $220,000 to $245,000 of value depending on how the split falls. At a combined millage rate of roughly 1.5%, that saves you $375 to $750 per year.

More importantly, the Save Our Homes amendment caps how fast your assessed value can increase each year at 3% or the rate of inflation, whichever is lower. This means if the market runs up, your tax bill does not explode. This is a major benefit that renters do not get and that many out-of-state buyers do not realize exists.

Filing is done through your county property appraiser's office. It is free and only needs to be done once.

Florida-Specific Costs That Catch First-Time Buyers Off Guard

Florida has some costs that buyers from other states do not always expect.

Homeowners insurance is the biggest one. Florida's insurance market has been under stress for several years. Many national carriers stopped writing new policies. The state-backed Citizens Insurance is available but has been moving policies to private carriers. Rates depend heavily on your roof age and construction type.

A general range for a 3-bedroom single-family home in Central Florida: $2,000 to $5,000 per year depending on age of home, location, roof condition, and wind mitigation features. Get at least 3 quotes before you close. Some buyers are surprised enough to walk away from a deal when they see insurance costs.

Roof age matters more in Florida than most states. Many insurance companies will not write or renew a policy on a home with a roof older than 15 years, or will charge significantly more. When you look at any home in Florida, roof condition and age is the first thing to check.

HOA fees apply to many Florida communities. Some are low ($30 to $50 per month for basic maintenance). Some are high ($200 to $400 per month in gated or amenity-rich communities). The listing will show the monthly HOA fee, but you also want to review the HOA financials for any pending assessments.

Flood insurance may be required if the property is in a FEMA Special Flood Hazard Area. This is in addition to regular homeowners insurance and can cost $1,000 to $3,000+ per year. Check the FEMA flood map for any property before you make an offer.

How to Choose Between Ocala and Orlando as a First-Time Buyer

Both markets serve different buyers well.

Ocala makes sense if you work remotely, work in Ocala, or prioritize getting the most house for your money. First-time buyers can get a 3 or 4 bedroom home in Marion Oaks or Silver Springs Shores for $230,000 to $280,000. That is a realistic purchase on a median income without stretching the budget.

Orlando makes sense if you work in the metro and need to commute, or if you want more employment options, nightlife, and urban amenities. The tradeoff is price. Entry-level homes in desirable Orange County suburbs start around $320,000 to $380,000. Less expensive areas in Osceola or Polk County (Kissimmee, Davenport) bring costs down to $280,000 to $340,000.

For first-time buyers on a tight budget, the Osceola and Polk County markets adjacent to Orlando offer a middle ground: closer to Orlando's job market but with more affordable prices than Orange County.

What the Process Looks Like Step by Step

Here is the standard path for a Florida first-time buyer.

1. Check your credit. Florida lenders want to see at least 620 to 640 for conventional loans. FHA loans can go lower. Pull your credit reports before you start and fix any errors.

2. Get pre-approved. Contact a lender and get a pre-approval letter before you start looking seriously. In today's market, sellers in Florida do not take offers without one.

3. Sign a buyer representation agreement. Florida law now requires a written agreement with your buyer's agent before they can show you homes. This is standard and spells out how the agent gets paid.

4. Start touring. I send you matches, we tour the ones that fit, and you identify what you like and do not like.

5. Make an offer. I run comparable sales, advise on price and terms, and submit the offer.

6. Inspection period. Florida contracts typically include a 10 to 15 day inspection period where you can cancel for any reason and get your deposit back. Use it.

7. Closing. Florida closings go through a title company, not an attorney. Closing takes 30 to 45 days from accepted offer in most cases.

From first contact to keys in your hand, expect 2 to 4 months in a normal market.

Common Questions

Conventional loans typically require 620 to 640 minimum. FHA loans can go as low as 580 with 3.5% down, or 500 with 10% down. The Florida Housing Finance Corporation programs generally require a 640 or higher. The higher your score, the better your rate.

Florida Housing programs allow as little as 3% down on conventional loans or 3.5% on FHA. With down payment assistance stacked on top, some buyers close with very little out of pocket beyond closing costs. Closing costs in Florida typically run 2% to 3% of the purchase price.

Florida Housing income limits vary by county and household size. In Marion County (Ocala), the limit for a 1-2 person household is roughly $90,000 to $100,000. In Orange County (Orlando), limits are slightly higher to reflect higher local incomes. Your lender will check current limits when you apply.

Having your own agent is strongly recommended, especially as a first-time buyer. The listing agent represents the seller. Without your own representation, you are navigating a high-stakes transaction alone against someone whose job is to get the best deal for the other side. In most Florida transactions, the seller covers the buyer's agent commission, so you typically get representation at no out-of-pocket cost.
