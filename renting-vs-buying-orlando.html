Renting vs Buying in Orlando, FL: How to Decide

There is no universally right answer. Here is what actually determines which one makes sense for where you are right now.

The Honest Version of This Comparison

Every financial article on the internet tells you that buying builds equity and renting is throwing money away. That framing is oversimplified and ignores the specifics of your situation.

Renting can absolutely be the smarter financial move, depending on how long you plan to stay, what you can afford for a down payment, what the rental market looks like versus the purchase market, and what your life looks like over the next three to five years.

Buying builds equity and locks in a payment, but it also means property taxes, insurance, maintenance, and transaction costs when you eventually sell. If you buy and sell within two years, you almost always lose money net of closing costs on both ends.

Here is the framework I give people: if you are staying in Orlando for at least three to five years and can afford the full monthly cost of ownership, buying usually makes sense. If your timeline is shorter, or if the monthly cost of owning would strain your budget, renting while you build your position is the smarter move.

What Renting Costs in Orlando Right Now

The Orlando rental market has been one of the fastest-appreciating in the country. Rents rose sharply from 2021 to 2023 and have stabilized somewhat since then, but they are still significantly higher than they were in 2019.

A one-bedroom apartment in most Orlando neighborhoods runs $1,400 to $1,800 per month. Two bedrooms run $1,700 to $2,300. Downtown and Lake Nona are on the higher end. Areas like Kissimmee, Davenport, and parts of East Orlando offer lower rents for comparable space.

Ocala is considerably more affordable, with one-bedrooms starting around $1,100 to $1,400. If you work remotely or your job is in Ocala, the rental cost difference between Orlando and Ocala is significant.

Rent does not build equity, but it also does not lock you into a location, a maintenance responsibility, or a specific payment for 30 years. That flexibility has real value depending on where you are in life.

What Buying Costs in Orlando Right Now

The Orlando purchase market has been competitive since 2020. Median home prices in Orange County sit in the $380,000 to $420,000 range as of 2026. That is lower than Tampa but higher than many other Florida metros.

On a $380,000 home with 5% down and a 30-year mortgage at current rates, your principal and interest payment alone is roughly $2,100 to $2,300 per month. Add property taxes ($350 to $500/month), homeowners insurance ($200 to $300/month), and any HOA fee, and your total monthly cost is typically $2,800 to $3,400 or more.

That is meaningfully higher than renting a comparable space in most Orlando neighborhoods. The trade is that your payment is fixed, you build equity as you pay down the loan and the value appreciates, and you have stability.

For buyers focused on affordability, Ocala changes the math significantly. A $270,000 home in Silver Springs Shores with 5% down carries a total monthly cost closer to $1,900 to $2,200, which is competitive with or even below what renting a two-bedroom in Orlando costs.

When Renting Makes More Sense

Renting is the smarter choice if any of these apply to your situation.

You are staying less than two to three years. Transaction costs on buying and selling a home in Florida total 8 to 10% of the purchase price between closing costs and real estate commissions. You need appreciation or a long time horizon to overcome that math.

Your down payment is not ready. Stretching to put 3% down while your savings are thin creates risk. If something breaks in year one, if you lose income for a few months, you need a cushion. Buying before you have one is how people end up in financial trouble.

Your life situation is uncertain. Job change on the horizon, relationship status in flux, not sure which city you want to be in. Renting is the right tool for uncertain timelines.

The monthly cost would be a stretch. If buying means your housing costs are more than 30 to 35% of your gross income, you are probably overextending. Run the full math including taxes, insurance, and maintenance, not just the mortgage payment.

When Buying Makes More Sense

Buying makes more sense when your situation looks like this.

You are staying at least three to five years. The longer you hold a home, the more transaction costs get offset by equity built and appreciation. Three to five years is the minimum horizon where buying usually beats renting in Central Florida.

You have the down payment and a reserve. At minimum, 3 to 5% down plus enough cash left over to cover 2 to 3 months of expenses. Ideally 10 to 20% down to avoid PMI and have a lower payment.

Your total monthly housing cost is manageable. Run the full number: mortgage, taxes, insurance, HOA. If it is under 30 to 35% of your gross income, you are in a sustainable range.

You want stability. A fixed mortgage payment is a hedge against the rental market. Rents in Orlando have risen significantly. A fixed payment locks in your housing cost for 30 years, which has compounding value over time.
