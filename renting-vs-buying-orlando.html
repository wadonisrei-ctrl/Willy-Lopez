Renting vs. Buying in Orlando, FL: An Honest Look

The right answer depends on your timeline, finances, and what you want out of where you live. Here is the real breakdown for 2025.

The Numbers Side of the Question

Let us start with a straight comparison of what renting and buying actually cost in Orlando right now.

Renting a 2-bedroom apartment in a mid-tier Orlando community runs $1,700 to $2,100 per month. Over 12 months, that is $20,400 to $25,200 paid with nothing to show for it at the end.

Buying a 3-bedroom single-family home in Orange County at the median price of around $380,000 with 10% down ($38,000) and a 7% interest rate produces a principal and interest payment of roughly $2,400 per month. Add property taxes (roughly $400 per month on a $380,000 home) and homeowners insurance ($250 to $400 per month, depending on the property), and your total monthly housing cost lands around $3,000 to $3,200.

On a straight monthly payment comparison, renting is cheaper right now. But that is only half the picture.

When you buy, part of every payment goes to principal reduction (building equity). You get the homestead exemption, capping future tax increases. And if Orlando's market appreciates even modestly, a $380,000 home at 3% annual appreciation becomes a $440,000 home in 5 years, netting you roughly $60,000 in equity gain on top of what you paid down.

When you rent, you pay and the landlord builds equity.

When Renting Makes More Sense

Renting is the smarter choice in specific situations.

If your timeline is under 2 to 3 years, the math usually favors renting. Buying and selling a home costs 6 to 8% of the purchase price between agent commissions, closing costs, and moving expenses. If you are not staying long enough to recoup those costs through appreciation and equity paydown, renting is cheaper.

If your credit or down payment is not ready yet, renting while you build both is better than stretching into a purchase you cannot sustain. A 620 credit score and 3.5% down is technically possible with FHA, but a higher score and larger down payment get you a meaningfully better rate.

If your income is not stable, renting offers flexibility that buying does not. You can move, downsize, or relocate on 30 to 60 days notice. A mortgage is a 30-year commitment.

If you are new to the Orlando area and still figuring out where you want to live, renting for a year gives you time to learn the neighborhoods, commute patterns, and community feel before you commit to one. I have helped people rent in one area and then buy in a completely different neighborhood once they knew the market.

When Buying Makes More Sense

Buying tends to win when your situation has stability.

If you are staying for 5 or more years, the long-term math favors buying in Orlando. Appreciation historically outpaces rental increases over long time horizons. And every month of mortgage payments builds equity that stays with you.

If you have kids in school and value stability, buying locks in your community and your school district. Renting means your landlord can raise rent, sell the property, or decide not to renew your lease.

If renting feels pointless to you, it is a valid instinct. You can build wealth renting, but it requires investing the difference in rent vs. ownership elsewhere. Most people do not. Buying forces a form of savings through equity accumulation that renting does not.

If you want control over your space, buying gives it to you. You can paint, remodel, get any pet you want, and make the place your own without asking permission.

A Third Option: Ocala

A lot of people come to this question thinking the choice is rent in Orlando or buy in Orlando. There is a third path worth considering.

Buy in Ocala instead.

Ocala is about 80 miles north of Orlando. That is too far for daily commuting, but if you work remotely or have a job in Ocala, the comparison changes completely.

In Ocala, a 3 or 4 bedroom home in a solid neighborhood runs $250,000 to $300,000. At 7% with 10% down, your principal and interest payment is roughly $1,600 to $1,950. Add taxes and insurance and you are at $2,200 to $2,600 total monthly. That is competitive with renting a 2-bedroom in Orlando, and you own a house.

For remote workers and people who do not need to be physically in Orlando, Ocala is worth a look. I work both markets and can give you an honest comparison.

FAQ

Common Questions

On a monthly payment basis, renting is cheaper in 2025 because of elevated interest rates. A 2-bedroom rental runs $1,700 to $2,100 per month. Buying a 3-bedroom home at median Orlando prices with 7% financing runs $3,000 to $3,200 per month all-in. But buying builds equity and protects you from future rent increases, so it is not purely a monthly comparison.

Generally 3 to 5 years minimum to break even on buying costs (closing costs, agent commissions, mortgage interest in early years). If you plan to stay 5 or more years, buying becomes increasingly advantageous over renting as equity builds and you capture any appreciation.

You can go as low as 3% down on a conventional loan or 3.5% with FHA. On a $380,000 home, 3% is $11,400. However, going below 20% means you will pay private mortgage insurance (PMI), which adds $100 to $200 per month to your payment. Florida Housing Finance programs can also help first-time buyers with down payment assistance.

It depends how long that saving will take. If you can realistically buy within 12 to 18 months, renting while you save makes sense. If buying is 3 or more years away, consider whether there is a market or price range where you could buy sooner with what you have now (Ocala, Kissimmee, or Davenport are often more accessible than core Orange County).

At current Orlando prices and rates, the typical break-even is 4 to 5 years assuming modest appreciation (3% annually). Before that point, your transaction costs have not been recovered. After that point, total cost of homeownership starts to fall below what you would have paid in rent.
