I am a licensed real estate agent based in Central Florida, and I list homes in both the Greater Orlando area and Ocala. Covering both markets means I can give you an honest read on where your home fits, whether you are selling to move across town or relocating out of the area entirely.

Orlando vs Ocala: Two Different Seller Markets

Orlando and Ocala sell differently, and knowing the difference matters for how we price and market your home.

Orlando (Greater Metro) has a deeper buyer pool and more competition among listings. Per the Orlando Regional Realtor Association, the metro median sale price has been running roughly $370,000 to $400,000 through 2026. Well-priced homes in strong submarkets like Lake Nona, Winter Park, and Dr. Phillips still move quickly. Overpriced homes sit longer than they would have a few years ago, since buyers have more inventory to compare against.

Ocala is a more affordable market with recent reports putting the Marion County median in the $270,000 to $305,000 range. The Ocala metro has also been named the fastest-growing metro in the country in recent U.S. Census Bureau population estimates, which has kept buyer demand steady across price tiers. Cash offers are more common here than in Orlando, especially for entry-level and retiree-demographic homes.

If you own in one market and are moving to or from the other, I can walk you through both sides of the transaction.

How I Price Your Home

Pricing strategy works the same in both markets: the comps decide, not the wish. I pull the recent sales in your immediate neighborhood, adjust for condition, square footage, upgrades, and lot, and hand you a number I can defend line by line.

What I will not do is anchor to a Zestimate, which has never walked through your house, or quote you a flattering figure just to win the listing. Buyer attention peaks in a listing's first two weeks. Homes priced to the comps capture that window. Homes priced to the seller's hopes burn it, then take a visible price cut that makes the next round of buyers wonder what is wrong with the house.

What the Selling Process Looks Like in Florida

The legal mechanics are the same statewide, whether the house sits in Orange County or Marion County.

Florida's disclosure law puts the burden on you as the seller: any known material defect a buyer could not easily spot, roof, plumbing, past flooding, goes on the disclosure form. Done properly, that form is legal protection, not a liability.

Two closing costs tend to surprise first-time sellers. The state's documentary stamp tax runs $0.70 per $100 of the sale price, which is $2,450 on a $350,000 home. And in most Florida closings the seller pays for the owner's title insurance policy, priced at the state-promulgated rate, so it will not vary based on which title company handles it.

On timing, a financed buyer usually needs 30 to 45 days from contract to closing, while cash can be done in as little as 10 to 21 days. That difference matters more in Ocala, where cash offers are a bigger share of the market.

How Commission Works

There is nothing to pay me up front. My fee comes out of the proceeds at the closing table.

The rate itself is negotiated per listing, not fixed. And since the NAR settlement took effect in August 2024, any compensation offered to a buyer's agent is a separate, explicit decision we make together based on your goals and what the current market expects, not an automatic add-on.

Before a listing agreement gets signed, you will have the full picture in plain numbers: my side, the buyer-agent side if any, and what that leaves you at your likely sale price.
