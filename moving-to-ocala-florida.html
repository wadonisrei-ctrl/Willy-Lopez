Why People Move to Ocala

Most people moving to Ocala are coming from one of three places: South Florida, the Northeast, or the Midwest. The reasons are usually the same. Florida has no state income tax, which by itself makes a real difference for middle-income earners. Ocala rents run well below Miami, Fort Lauderdale, and Orlando. From what I see day to day, more people are moving to Marion County every year, and the pace of life is noticeably slower than the big metros.

A lot of the growth is retirees and horse industry professionals. Ocala calls itself the horse capital of the world, and the equestrian economy around it brings trainers, vets, and farm staff from all over the country. But it is not just horse people anymore. Remote workers and healthcare workers make up a growing share of new arrivals, drawn by the lower cost of living and two major hospital systems.

If you are coming from South Florida specifically, the price shock goes the other direction in the best way. A two-bedroom in Ocala can rent for what you might pay for a studio in Fort Lauderdale. That difference adds up fast, especially if you are on a fixed income or just starting out.

How Ocala Compares to Other Florida Cities

Ocala sits in North Central Florida, about 90 minutes from Orlando and Jacksonville and roughly 2 hours from Tampa. It is inland, so there is no beach commute to factor in, but the tradeoff is a noticeably lower cost of living and less traffic than any major Florida metro.

Compared to Orlando, Ocala has smaller rental inventory but much lower average rents. Compared to Gainesville, Ocala has less of a college-town feel and more of a working, family-oriented market. Compared to The Villages just to the south, Ocala has more variety in age and lifestyle, not just a retirement community.

Most people who contact me and say they are moving to Ocala are relocating for a job at one of the two hospital systems, a horse farm position, or simply chasing a lower cost of living while staying within driving distance of Orlando and Gainesville.

What to Know About the Ocala Rental Market Before You Move

Ocala is still one of the more affordable rental markets in Florida, but it is not as cheap as it was a few years ago. Like most of the state, Marion County has seen more people moving in since 2020, and new construction has not fully caught up with demand yet.

Based on the communities I actually work with, a realistic budget for a one-bedroom is $1,349 and up per month. Two-bedrooms run $1,375 to $1,519 and up depending on the community. If you need something below that price point, or a studio, I do not currently have a match, and I will tell you that directly rather than string you along.

Move-in costs are usually one to two months of rent upfront. That typically includes a deposit and first month. Some communities offer reduced deposits for strong applicants, and a few waive the deposit entirely during lease-up specials on new construction.

For income requirements, most communities require gross monthly income of 2.5 to 3 times the monthly rent. If you are a remote worker, freelancer, or otherwise self-employed, bring 2 to 3 months of bank statements. Communities accept those in place of traditional pay stubs.

If you have a pet, expect a non-refundable pet fee of $200 to $500 and a monthly pet rent of $25 to $75 per pet. Most communities have weight limits, usually 50 to 75 pounds, and restrict certain breeds. I filter for pet-friendly communities based on your specific pet.
