Moving to Ocala, Florida

Why People Move to Ocala

Most people moving to Ocala are coming from one of three places: South Florida, the Northeast, or the Midwest. The reasons are usually the same. Florida has no state income tax, which by itself makes a real difference for middle-income earners. Ocala rents run well below Miami, Fort Lauderdale, and Orlando. From what I see day to day, more people are moving to Marion County every year, and the pace of life is noticeably slower than the big metros.

A lot of the growth is retirees and horse industry professionals. Ocala calls itself the horse capital of the world, and the equestrian economy around it brings trainers, vets, and farm staff from all over the country. But it is not just horse people anymore. Remote workers and healthcare workers make up a growing share of new arrivals, drawn by the lower cost of living and two major hospital systems.

If you are coming from South Florida specifically, the price shock goes the other direction in the best way. A two-bedroom in Ocala can rent for what you might pay for a studio in Fort Lauderdale. That difference adds up fast, especially if you are on a fixed income or just starting out.

How Ocala Compares to Other Florida Cities

Ocala sits in North Central Florida, about 90 minutes from Orlando and Jacksonville and roughly 2 hours from Tampa. It is inland, so there is no beach commute to factor in, but the tradeoff is a noticeably lower cost of living and less traffic than any major Florida metro.

Compared to Orlando, Ocala has smaller rental inventory but much lower average rents. Compared to Gainesville, Ocala has less of a college-town feel and more of a working, family-oriented market. Compared to The Villages just to the south, Ocala has more variety in age and lifestyle, not just a retirement community.

Most people who contact me and say they are moving to Ocala are relocating for a job at one of the two hospital systems, a horse farm position, or simply chasing a lower cost of living while staying within driving distance of Orlando and Gainesville.

Your Florida Move-In Checklist

Once you have a move date, a few Florida-specific tasks need to happen that people moving within-state do not think about. Florida requires new residents to get a Florida driver's license and register any vehicles, generally within 30 days of establishing residency. The DMV office on SW 19th Avenue Road handles both in Ocala, and a signed lease is normally accepted as proof of Florida residency.

Utilities depend on exactly where you land. Inside Ocala city limits, electricity runs through Ocala Electric Utility, a city-owned utility. Outside city limits in Marion County, it is usually Duke Energy or SECO Energy depending on the area. Water and sewer inside city limits go through the City of Ocala; some outlying areas are on well and septic instead, which your leasing office can confirm.

Update your address with USPS mail forwarding, your bank, and any subscriptions before the move, not after. If you want to register to vote in Florida, Marion County's Supervisor of Elections office handles that once you have a Florida address.

None of this needs to happen before you arrive. It needs to happen in the first 30 days after you do. The one thing worth handling before you arrive is the apartment itself, so you are not doing all of this from a hotel room.

Your Moving Timeline: What to Handle and When

6 to 8 weeks out: narrow down a budget and area. This is when I usually hear from people first. Tell me your move date, budget, and bedroom count, and I will tell you honestly whether the communities I work with in NW or SW Ocala fit, or point you elsewhere in Central Florida if they do not.

4 weeks out: get your apartment locked in. Once you pick a community, you can complete a video walkthrough, apply, and get approved remotely in most cases. Most approvals come back within 24 to 48 hours. Book your moving company or truck rental now if you have not already, since good movers book up fast in summer.

2 weeks out: start the address change list. USPS mail forwarding, banks, subscriptions, and any medical providers who need records transferred.

Moving week: keep your lease, ID, and proof of income documents with you, not packed in the moving truck. You will want them on hand for the move-in walkthrough and utility setup.

First 30 days after arrival: Florida driver's license, vehicle registration, and voter registration if you want it done. See the checklist above for where to go.

I only handle one piece of this personally, the apartment. But getting that piece locked in early takes the biggest source of moving stress off the table before you deal with everything else.

Cost of Living: Ocala vs Where You're Coming From

Rent is usually the biggest line item people compare, but it is not the only one that shifts when you move to Florida. No state income tax is the headline difference, and for most middle-income earners it is a real, immediate raise compared to states like New York, Illinois, or California.

Home and auto insurance run higher in Florida than in most of the country, largely because of statewide hurricane and flood risk. It is worth budgeting for this rather than being surprised by it after you sign a lease. Renters insurance itself is usually inexpensive, often under $20 a month, and some of the communities I work with require it.

Groceries and everyday goods in Ocala run close to the national average, sometimes slightly below, since it is not a tourist-driven market the way Orlando or Miami are. Gas prices track the statewide average.
